Sustainable Investing, What It Is and What It Isn't

A Look into ESG funds, their investments, and whether they are achieving the goals that you want them to.

October 5, 2020

According in MorningStar, as of the end of June, sustainable investment funds, or ESG funds were managing 250 billion dollars in assetssup1. ESG stands for Environmental, Social, and Corporate Governance tends to be the type of fund people refer to when they are talking about sustainable investments. Often times these are "Exclusionary" funds, which seek to remove investment assets which score poorly in the ESG ratings

How are Stocks rated for ESG?

One of the "big" ratings agencies for the ESG worthiness of a stock or a fund is MSCI, which from their website "strive[s] to bring greater transparency to financial markets, enabling the investment community to make better decisions for a better world." They might not be the only reliable source for quality ESG ratings, but in my research they came up time and time again. Their tool allows you to search for an individual stock and see where they are leaders in an area normally deemed important to sustainable investing, and where they lag behind their industry competitors, as well as view their rating which follows a similar order as other ratings companies use for bonds (AAA is the best followed by AA, all the way down to CCC)2. This works very well for investigating individual stocks, but is a little less clear cut when you start looking into mutual funds and ETFs.

Until ESG funds started doing well during the pandemic and they switched to charts showing upward trends, the imagery around ESG funds is always very green and emoting a sense of sustainability in the most traditional sense. Source: CultureTalk

Before we look into exactly what you'll find in an ESG fund, I want you to consider what you or a typical investor who is drawn to sustainable investing might want to be investing in and what their goals are. Of course their goal is to make money, it is an investment after all, but probably also to invest in things that are more good than evil, to put their money where their mouth is when they say that want to stop climate change and social injustice. They certainly are advertised as such by many popular media sources3. One might want to offload the work of finding which renewable resource companies are actually making things work, and which have weak business models. Perhaps, and these are the people who will be least surprised by the assets found in traditional ESG funds, they want a fund that looks a lot like other Growth Stock funds, but with a few things removed.

Are there any you are surprised to find? I was surprised that every single one of the etfs have significant assets in Amazon, Apple and Facebook (Microsoft and Google I — perhaps unjustly — tend to consider less evil). To argue that "ESG" funds are beating the market is simply to say that if you heavily invest in major tech firms that were somehow deemed sustainable, socially good, with transparent and just corporate governance, despite their sketchy records, then you'll beat the market when tech giants are bullish.

"Even where an ESG index did beat the market, it had little to do with environmental, social or governance issues. Instead, it came down to luck: did they happen to pick the stocks that best rode out coronavirus lockdowns? It is better to be lucky than right; but having, as some did, less exposure to cruise liners or long-haul airlines because of their carbon footprint was luck, not a well-thought-out way to avoid the stocks hurt most by Covid-19. There are several reasons why Microsoft tends to score well on ESG, but its cloud services being in demand because everyone is working from home isn’t among them."6

It should also be noted that for nearly exactly the same fund, Vanguard's expense ration on their ESG fund is 0.12%, where as for their Growth fund it is 0.04%. Of course neither of those are extremely high because in general Vanguard is known for their low fees, but it still a 200% difference.

Conclusions

When I first had been hearing that more and more people were investing in ESG funds while simultaneously these funds were beginning to beat the market, I became skeptical. What I discovered, that they tend to be heavily invested in FAA(N)G stocks, just like everyone else, was different that my first suspicion. What I'd first suspected was that as a result of an influx of cash into the stocks that were deemed sustainable, their prices were being artificially inflated due to demand and excitement7. As it is, this suspicion has been proven false.

If the investor's goal in choosing an ESG fund is to mirror an normal growth or index fund but to not invest in a few specific companies are are dumping oil in the sea, or have major corporate governance scandals actively being investigated, then ESG funds make a lot of sense. If however an investor's goal is to put money into causes they believe in, further research is definitely in order.
